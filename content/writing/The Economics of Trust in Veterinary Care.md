Reports over the past year have highlighted a sharp increase in veterinary fees across the United Kingdom, alongside a wave of consolidation in which independent practices have been absorbed by large corporate groups. The Competition and Markets Authority has now [completed a review into the sector](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cj32d05x702o), and announced a series of measures, including a requirement for practices to publish price lists and the introduction of a price comparison website intended to strengthen competition and place downward pressure on costs.

At the level of everyday customer experience, very little appears to have changed. A visit to a veterinary practice follows a familiar pattern. The waiting room, the consultation, and the vet's  tone of professional reassurance remain much the same. Most owners continue to behave as they did when practices were locally owned: they defer to the vet’s judgement and accept recommendations as necessary. They tend to treat cost as secondary to care.

But  that pattern of behaviour was formed in a very different economic setting. Veterinary care belongs to a class of services in which the customer cannot easily judge what is required, whether it has been delivered optimally, or whether a cheaper alternative would have been sufficient. Economists describe these as ***credence goods***. The provider identifies the problem, proposes the solution, and carries it out, all within the same interaction. In such markets, verification is difficult and often delayed and from necessity trust substitutes for direct evaluation. The owner relies on the vet’s recommendation because there is no straightforward way to assess it independently. Trust in this context functions as a practical shortcut. It reduces the need to question each step, allowing decisions to be made quickly under conditions of uncertainty. 

That shortcut made sense in a system where incentives were relatively simple and where long-term relationships between owner and practitioner were common. It reduced the transaction costs associated with seeking second opinions, comparing treatments, or delaying action. It also aligned with the emotional reality of veterinary care, where the perceived cost of hesitation can feel higher than the financial cost of treatment.

Ownership structures have changed. Large corporate groups now control a significant share of practices, introducing centralised pricing, standardised treatment pathways, and explicit revenue targets. These developments have left the immediate experience of the consultation largely intact, with the direct customer-vet interaction still apparently a straightforward exercise of clinical judgement. But the real change lies in the way recommendations are organised and priced. Diagnostic steps, follow-up procedures, and medication plans are increasingly arranged into defined sequences to maximise revenues, with each step carrying associated costs that build over the course of the visit.

The behaviour of owners has not adjusted to reflect the pace of this quite sudden change. During a consultation, agreement typically occurs in real time. Questions focus on the animal’s condition and likely outcome. Cost is often considered after the fact, once the invoice is reviewed but by that point, the treatment has already been carried out and the opportunity to compare options or to refuse elements of the plan has passed. The timing of the decision matters. It reflects the way trust operates within the interaction: it encourages acceptance at precisely the moment when alternatives are hardest to evaluate.

This dynamic can also be understood through the ***principal–agent framework***. The owner delegates decision-making authority to the vet, who possesses greater expertise. In many settings, this delegation is moderated by monitoring or by the ability to compare providers. In veterinary care, both are limited. The owner cannot easily assess the necessity of each intervention, and the cost of seeking alternatives is high in terms of time, stress, and perceived risk to the animal. 

Demand in this setting is only weakly responsive to price. The health of a pet carries emotional weight, and decisions are often made under time pressure. These factors reduce price sensitivity. Owners are less likely to question or delay treatment when doing so feels as though it might compromise care. Trust reinforces this effect by making acceptance feel appropriate and questioning feel misplaced. The combination of emotional urgency and informational asymmetry produces a form of demand in which price plays a secondary role at the point of decision.

The structure of pricing itself further limits comparison. Charges are often revealed after agreement, and services are bundled into treatment pathways that are not easily disaggregated. This reduces the visibility of individual costs and makes it difficult to compare across providers. In standard markets, price discovery depends on transparency and the ability to evaluate alternatives. In this context, both are constrained. Trust fills the gap, allowing decisions to proceed without the need for explicit comparison.

These features create a lag between changes in the system and changes in behaviour. Owners continue to act on assumptions formed under the old model of provision, while the underlying incentives have shifted. The consultation feels the same, and the interaction proceeds in the same way. The difference becomes visible only when the accumulated cost is reviewed, and even then it does not necessarily translate into different behaviour at the next visit. The habits of trust remain in place.

None of this requires bad faith on the part of individual practitioners. Vets continue to operate within professional norms, and many remain committed to the welfare of the animals they treat - but the system in which they work has changed. Pricing is now set within a broader organisational framework, and treatment pathways are shaped by protocols that extend beyond the individual practice. 

Regulatory measures may improve visibility, though visibility alone does not alter behaviour at the moment decisions are made. The consultation still encourages acceptance, and the cost is still realised afterwards. As long as that sequence holds, the relationship between trust and price will remain intact.

A system in which trust once *reduced* the need for scrutiny now *relies* on that same absence of scrutiny, even as the underlying incentives have changed, because customer behaviour has not caught up with the changed reality.
